Intellectual Property Law, particularly in the United States, is one of the only ways for innovators and new businesses to create a competitive advantage or legal monopoly, where companies that already have distribution would otherwise dominate. Under the broader umbrella of IP law are copyright, trade secrets, fair use, and patents. Each category has distinct legal definitions and purposes to protect or allow the usage of other people's creations. As Sara Baase and Timothy Henry outline in [A Gift of Fire: Social, Legal, and Ethical Issues for Computing Technology](https://www.pearson.com/en-us/subject-catalog/p/gift-of-fire-social-legal-and-ethical-issues-for-computing-technology/P200000003391), the arrival of software and the developer community raised many challenges to the traditional meanings of intellectual property law, since the difficulty of copying and distributing intellectual property was much lower than it had ever been.

The case of [United States v. Aleynikov](https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/11-1126/11-1126-2012-04-11.html) is an interesting example of such challenges. Sergey Aleynikov copied roughly 32 megabytes — approximately 500,000 lines — of source code from Goldman Sachs that was used in the firm's high-frequency trading platform. In his last days at the firm in June 2009, he encrypted the files and uploaded them to a Subversion repository hosted on a server in Germany before downloading them to personal devices. At the time, copying code was a common practice in the software community, and Aleynikov was not alone in such practices. Michael Lewis describes the broader context in [Vanity Fair’s account of the case](https://www.vanityfair.com/news/2013/09/michael-lewis-goldman-sachs-programmer). However, Aleynikov’s encryption of the files and his deletion of his bash command history indicated that he understood the code was not his to take. In his legal defense, Aleynikov and his lawyer argued that he copied the bundled files with the intention of later extracting the open-source components he had worked with at Goldman — in service of his new job at Teza Technologies — rather than to steal the proprietary code owned by Goldman Sachs.

The fair use doctrine did not cover Aleynikov's actions, and his lawyers did not pursue that defense. Fair use is a copyright doctrine intended to permit limited, typically transformative uses of protected work that serve innovation, commentary, education, or the general betterment of society. Under [17 U.S.C. § 107](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/17/107), courts evaluate fair use through a four-factor test that weighs the purpose and character of the use, the nature of the original work, the amount and substantiality of what was copied, and the effect on the market for the original. [Campbell v. Acuff-Rose Music, Inc.](https://supreme.justia.com/cases/federal/us/510/569/) is one of the major Supreme Court cases explaining that analysis. Aleynikov's conduct fails on every factor: his use was commercial and non-transformative, since he intended to deploy similar code for the same high-frequency trading purpose at a direct competitor; Goldman's platform was a highly creative, unpublished proprietary work; the copying was substantial in both quantity and qualitative importance; and any deployment at Teza would have directly harmed Goldman's competitive market position.

Instead, the prosecution charged Aleynikov with theft of trade secrets — commercially valuable information that a company has taken reasonable steps to keep secret, and which is protected without ever being patented or copyrighted. Federal law covers this through the [Economic Espionage Act](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/1832), and later civil enforcement was expanded through the [Defend Trade Secrets Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/114th-congress/senate-bill/1890). Aleynikov did take the code, and his conduct constituted theft of trade secrets. However, his treatment and the swiftness of the prosecution were a symptom of the institutional power of Goldman Sachs rather than the severity of his crime.

Goldman reported the suspected breach to the FBI on July 1, 2009, and federal agents arrested Aleynikov just two days later at Newark Liberty International Airport. Over the course of the following decade, and more than three million dollars in legal fees, Aleynikov saw his federal convictions reversed by the Second Circuit and most of his state charges dismissed. Bloomberg reported on the end of the prosecution in [Ex-Goldman Programmer Aleynikov Sentenced to Time Served](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-10-23/ex-goldman-programmer-aleynikov-sentenced-to-time-served). It took appeals, approximately a full year served in federal prison, and a second arrest by the State of New York for Aleynikov to make it through the legal challenges; he was ultimately convicted on a single New York state count, but sentenced only to time served at the prosecution's own recommendation.

He lost his money, his marriage, and a decade of his life over code that he never deployed and never profited from. While his conduct may not have been completely ethical, it is worth noting that Aleynikov was the only Goldman Sachs employee arrested in the fallout of the 2008 financial crisis — and the code he wrote and copied had little to do with the conduct that caused that crisis. Michael Lewis’s book [Flash Boys: A Wall Street Revolt](https://wwnorton.com/books/Flash-Boys/) places the case within a larger critique of high-frequency trading and financial infrastructure.

Aleynikov copied roughly 32 megabytes of code, around 500,000 lines, much of which sat atop the open-source libraries that Goldman's HFT platform was largely built upon — although the Second Circuit found that a substantially greater number of the uploaded files contained proprietary code. By contrast, Anthony Levandowski, Google's former self-driving car engineer, downloaded 9.7 gigabytes of confidential data, founded a company that sold to Uber for $680 million, and was sentenced to only 18 months in prison, as reported by [The New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/08/04/technology/anthony-levandowski-google-uber-sentenced.html). The contrast between these cases is stark and shows the disparity in how copying trade secrets in the software industry is handled. Much of it comes down to the institutional power and vindictiveness of the company that owns the trade secrets in question.

The outcome — conviction on a single state count with no additional prison time — was the most just resolution available within a flawed legal framework, though the decade-long ordeal itself reflects a systemic problem. Since Aleynikov's federal acquittal, the laws he was prosecuted under have been reformed to better account for the copying of internally used software. Congress passed the [Theft of Trade Secrets Clarification Act of 2012](https://www.congress.gov/bill/112th-congress/senate-bill/3642) within months of the Second Circuit ruling, broadening the Economic Espionage Act to cover trade secrets used in — not only produced for — interstate commerce, and later passed the Defend Trade Secrets Act of 2016 to create a federal civil cause of action for trade secret misappropriation. Not much, though, has been done about the institutional leverage that major financial institutions wield with effective impunity.
