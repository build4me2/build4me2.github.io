Technology is not neutral. It encodes the values, incentives, and blind spots of the people and institutions that fund it, build it, deploy it, and profit from it. That is why ethical software development cannot be reduced to asking whether a tool is impressive, innovative, or technically legal. The more important question is: **what kind of world does this system make easier to govern, surveil, censor, or resist?**

In computer science, law often arrives late. New platforms and tools can grow faster than regulation, enter gray legal zones, and become so socially embedded that removing them becomes politically difficult. {{< cite "Transcript of Peter Thiel on Conversations with Bill Kristol" "https://conversationswithbillkristol.org/transcript/peter-thiel-transcript/" "Bill Kristol" "2018" >}} That delay creates an ethical burden for engineers. If the courts, legislatures, and policies cannot define the boundary in time, then developers are not only writing code. They are helping write the practical rules of social life.

## Ethical Frameworks Are Useful, But Not Enough

Utilitarianism asks us to maximize overall well-being. That sounds attractive in technology, especially when companies claim their products will help everyone. But with software, the downstream effects are often impossible to calculate at launch. AI tools may expand access to knowledge, but they can also increase energy use, weaken education when used carelessly, centralize power, and make surveillance more efficient. A utilitarian prediction can become optimism disguised as ethics.

Deontology, or duty-based ethics, is also limited. A company might argue that it has a duty to make AI freely available because knowledge should be shared. But “free” platforms often collect data, shape behavior, and move costs onto users, workers, communities, and the environment. A good principle can become harmful when applied without attention to infrastructure and consequences.

For technology, virtue ethics is often the most practical framework. Instead of only asking, “What rule applies?” or “What outcome do we predict?”, it asks: **what kind of engineer should I become?** In a field where innovation often outruns law, the character, courage, and judgment of builders matter. Engineers must develop the habit of asking whether their work expands human agency or concentrates power.

## Technology Companies Know Too Much About Us

Technology companies know too much about us because their business models often depend on knowing too much. Users technically agree to terms of service, but that consent is often semi-consensual: refusing may mean losing access to communication, work, education, or community. Even when data is shared with “trusted partners,” the fact that it is traded at all suggests it was collected for leverage beyond improving the user experience.

The privacy problem is also political. Governments are restricted in how they can surveil citizens directly, but corporations can collect data through contracts users accept. Governments can then benefit from private data ecosystems without carrying the same visible constitutional burden. The result is an inverted relationship: people create more personal data than ever before while having less real control over where it goes.

Regulations like the EU Digital Markets Act and Digital Services Act show that governments can pressure platforms, but regulation remains uneven and reactive. {{< cite "European Commission fines Apple and Meta for non-compliance with the DMA" "https://www.huntonprivacyblog.com/2025/04/25/european-commission-fines-apple-and-meta-for-non-compliance-with-the-dma/" "Hunton Andrews Kurth LLP" "2025" >}} {{< cite "Digital Services Act" "https://eur-lex.europa.eu/eli/reg/2022/2065/oj" "European Parliament and Council of the European Union" "2022" >}} The deeper issue is that personal data has become infrastructure for advertising, policing, political influence, and market power.

## Free Speech Is More Important Than Feeling Safe Online

Freedom of speech should not be redesigned around comfort. Feelings of safety change across time, culture, and politics. Rights should be grounded in principles that protect people even when the speaker is unpopular or offensive. That does not mean every post is protected: direct threats, exploitation, and criminal harm are different from speech that merely offends.

The harder modern question is not only whether someone is allowed to post. It is whether algorithms, moderation systems, governments, and platform incentives determine who is actually heard. A person may be “free” to speak while their words are buried by opaque ranking systems. In that case, speech exists formally but not meaningfully.

This is why online speech debates must include platform power. Governments with economic leverage can pressure companies into proactive censorship. Platforms can comply because the market is too valuable to lose. In countries with less leverage, moderation may be reactive instead of structural. Either way, rules of speech are increasingly shaped by private infrastructure rather than democratic accountability.

The TikTok controversy in the United States shows how national security language can obscure speech concerns. Claims about foreign influence mixed with anxiety over young people’s political views, including pro-Palestinian content. Meanwhile, reporting and civil-society research have documented platform moderation and suppression concerns around Palestine-related speech on other platforms as well. {{< cite "Meta's Broken Promises: Systemic Censorship of Palestine Content on Instagram and Facebook" "https://www.hrw.org/report/2023/12/21/metas-broken-promises/systemic-censorship-palestine-content-instagram-and" "Human Rights Watch" "2023" >}}

In the age of infinite scroll, free speech is not only a legal right. It is also a technical design problem.

## Code, Power, and Consequence

The ethical stakes of software are visible in intellectual property, surveillance, warfare, and platform governance. The prosecution of Sergey Aleynikov showed how code can be treated as corporate property with enormous legal consequences, even when software culture normalizes copying, reuse, and open-source collaboration. {{< cite "United States v. Aleynikov" "https://law.justia.com/cases/federal/appellate-courts/ca2/11-1126/11-1126-2012-04-11.html" "United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit" "2012" >}}

But the most urgent ethical issue is not only who owns code. It is what code is used to do.

AI and data systems are already used in warfare, immigration enforcement, policing, advertising, and social control. Systems such as Lavender and reports around AI-assisted targeting in Gaza show how automation can accelerate lethal decision-making. {{< cite "Lavender: The AI machine directing Israel's bombing spree in Gaza" "https://www.972mag.com/lavender-ai-israeli-army-gaza/" "+972 Magazine" "2024" >}} Palantir’s ImmigrationOS contract with ICE shows how data integration can scale state enforcement. {{< cite "ICE to Use ImmigrationOS by Palantir" "https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/" "American Immigration Council" "2025" >}}

For engineers, “just work ethically inside the system” is not always enough. Modern companies use compartmentalization, zero-trust information structures, and massive organizational hierarchies. A developer may not know how their small component fits into a larger surveillance or weapons system. At the same time, the most available and highest-paying jobs are often inside companies with military, policing, advertising, or data-brokerage incentives.

Still, ethical responsibility does not disappear because the system is complicated.

## The Duty Is To Build Alternatives

The engineer’s duty is not only to refuse harm. It is also to build alternatives.

That means supporting open-source infrastructure that no single corporation fully owns. It means privacy-preserving communication, encrypted tools, anonymous publishing, and censorship-resistant networks. It means education technology that empowers students instead of extracting attention. It means sustainable energy tools, better batteries, distributed grids, and technology that gives communities more autonomy instead of more dependency.

A company can be profitable and still contribute to the commons. A tool can be innovative without being extractive. A platform can be useful without turning every user into a data product. These models exist, but they require engineers, founders, researchers, and users to choose them deliberately.

The guiding question I keep returning to is this:

> **What is the net effect of my skills on the distribution of power in the world?**

If my work makes already-powerful institutions more powerful, more opaque, and less accountable, then technical success is not enough. If my work gives ordinary people more privacy, knowledge, mobility, safety, and self-determination, then it moves closer to the ethical future I want to help build.

The radical act is not only criticizing harmful systems. The radical act is building something that works for people who do not already have power — and putting it in their hands.
