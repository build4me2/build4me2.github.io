Students should use AI for their assignments or schoolwork. Students should use AI for their schoolwork, but the way they use it must be designed, taught, and assessed deliberately. Banning AI from classrooms is both impractical and a disservice to students entering a workforce where AI fluency is fast becoming a baseline expectation. The honest question is not whether to allow AI, but how to integrate it so students build genuine skill rather than outsource the thinking that schooling exists to develop. The case for integration starts with what AI actually is in practice. Tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and GitHub Copilot are embedded in professional workflows: lawyers use them to draft and analyze documents, engineers rely on them for code review and debugging, and scientists use them to summarize literature. A graduate who has never learned to prompt, evaluate, and edit AI output enters that workforce at a measurable disadvantage. Schools that pretend AI does not exist are not protecting academic integrity; they are training students for a world that no longer exists. But "use AI" is not a curriculum. The risk of unstructured use is real: students who let an AI write their essay learn neither writing nor critical thinking, and they often cannot tell when the AI has produced something subtly wrong. The solution is to redesign assignments around skills AI cannot replace, namely judgment, synthesis, originality, and the ability to verify and revise. A history assignment can require students to use AI for a first draft, then critique that draft against primary sources, documenting every error and unsupported claim. A coding course can treat AI as a pair programmer while testing comprehension through oral defenses or live problem-solving. The goal shifts from producing an artifact to demonstrating mastery of a process. Integration also means teaching AI literacy as a subject in its own right. Students need to understand how language models actually work: that they predict plausible text rather than retrieve verified facts, that they hallucinate confidently, and that their outputs reflect the biases and gaps in their training data. They need to learn prompt construction, source verification, and the ethics of attribution. These are not optional add-ons; they are the new core competencies of any knowledge worker, and they belong in the curriculum the way library research and citation practices once did. Assessment must change with the tools. Take-home essays alone no longer measure what they used to. Schools can rebalance toward in-class writing, oral examinations, project-based work with documented process, and portfolios that show iteration rather than just final products. This is more work for educators, but it produces a more accurate picture of what students actually understand, something that was already drifting out of focus before AI arrived. The deeper point is that education has always adapted to new tools, from calculators to the internet, and each transition produced the same panic followed by the same eventual integration. AI is a larger shift than those, but the principle holds. Students prepared for the changing workforce are not those shielded from AI; they are the ones taught to use it skeptically,

ethically, and skillfully. The classrooms that get this right will not be the ones with the strictest bans, but the ones with the clearest frameworks for thinking alongside machines. But the more I read it, the more it reads like a position written to offend no one. As a CS student, my real critique is that it doesn't go far enough. The biggest thing I'd change is the framing. The essay says students should be allowed to use AI. That's the wrong verb. By the time my class graduates, the assignments that matter shouldn't be ones AI is permitted in — they should be ones AI is required for, because the problem is too hard to solve without it. A take-home problem that a student can finish faster with LLM is a problem that wasn't ambitious enough to begin with. The real shift isn't "let students use AI carefully," it's "make the work hard enough that using AI carefully is the only way to finish it." The essay almost gets there when it talks about redesigning assessment, but it stops at oral defenses and portfolios. That's playing defense. The offensive move is redesigning what we ask students to do, not just how we verify they did it. Second, the essay treats this as another tool transition, like calculators or the internet, then admits "AI is a larger shift." It should have sat with that admission longer. Calculators didn't write your senior thesis or decide who gets a loan. LLMs touch every knowledge job and a lot of decisions about people's lives, which means CS ethics coursework needs more than a refresh it needs to confront the actual paradoxes my generation will inherit: bias in training data, concentration of power, AI in healthcare and criminal justice the wealth-gap question of who gets access, and the genuine potential to solve problems in economics, politics, and public health if we get this right. Educators have always been the cornerstone of civilization, so there is a lot at stake. The essay gestures at "ethics of attribution" and stops. Attribution is the easy part. Third, the essay places all the work on students and educators but ignores the fact that educators need to educate themselves, not just teach about it. Researchers and teachers spend close to half their time on email, grading, and other administrative work. Local AI tools can automate much of that, freeing up the hours actually needed for the harder/productive tasks: keeping up with what new technology means for all of us and redesigning coursework around it. It falls on educators to prepare students for the technology itself and, more importantly, for the ethical duties they will carry as engineers, the genuinely hard choices they will have to make inside an extractive capitalist system where incentives often do not align with people's wellbeing. Technology defines what kind of life people live, and we have already seen that tech is political and can be destructive. Students need to understand that and learn to use these tools with conscience, not just competence, so they leave school able to build technology that makes people's lives better, not just more efficient.

Could a human write a better response? Yes. The essay is competent, balanced, but forgettable, which is the shape arguments take when nobody is actually at risk in writing them. A human who has lived inside this transition, who is literate about the technology and whose life is visibly shaped by it, can write something much sharper. The difference is in the point of view. LLMs can argue the theory of AI in education cleanly, but theory and practice are different things, and only someone who has lived the practice can weigh them honestly.
