Reflection on the ethical codes/resources/discussions.

Reflection on the ethical codes/resources/discussions As an aspiring software engineer, I see my work as cumulative — every line of code, every system I help build, either contributes to a more equitable world or accelerates extraction, dehumanization, and wealth concentration. The ethical code that resonates with me most is the IEEE-CS/ACM Joint Code's first principle: software engineers shall act consistently with the public interest. The ACM Code's 1.1 "contribute to society and human well-being" and 1.2 avoid harm" sit alongside it. I take these seriously, but I do not treat them as a checklist. Codes alone cannot tell you what to do inside a company whose incentives are misaligned with people's well-being, which falls on my research and personal judgment. If I were offered a role at a big tech company, my honest answer is that it depends on the company and my circumstances. For example, I would not work for Oracle, Uber, Tesla, or OpenAI; I would work for Anthropic. If I have an option, I would choose the opportunity that aligns with my values more and the kind of work I will be doing for them, as we discussed in class, choose one who is a little more ethical. If a project is tied directly to weapons, mass surveillance, illegal data harvesting, or any clearly destructive use, I would refuse. Outside those red lines, I would weigh the role against my financial stability. If taking it is what lets me build credibility and the leverage to later do the work I think is necessary, I would take it. But "taking it" does not mean staying silent. I would raise concerns where I can, learn and document everything to understand the system well enough to build alternatives, and pivot as soon as I have the means. The longer game, for me, is building technology that is accessible to all and makes their life at least a little easier. Only about sixteen percent of the world has used AI; the models are concentrated in a handful of companies, and the data centers that run them consume enormous amounts of energy and fresh water, costs that fall hardest on the same communities (Indigenous people, mining regions, women, and minorities) that big tech already treats as disposable. I want to use AI, but on different terms: local small models, decentralized and shared hardware, and tooling that does not depend on extractive data centers. I want to build open-source tools educators can adapt for their own classrooms, and tools aimed at open problems like clean energy transition, battery efficiency, afforestation siting, and medical research and advancement. Optimising AI tools is necessary for me to build efficiently and fast before we get left too behind to catch up or resist oligarchs. Using it responsibly, for me, means using it for the service of accessibility and accountability, not only profit generation. That is the ethical responsibility I want to carry into this profession.
