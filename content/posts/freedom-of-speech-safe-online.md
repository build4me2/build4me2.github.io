          “ Freedom of speech is more important than the ability to feel safe online.”



         The feelings and perceptions of individuals are subject to change with time as society

and cultures change, and thus, laws should not be shaped to accommodate comfort, but to

implement ethical principles that afford the basic rights of humans and are as universally

unchanging as best they can be. As the law lags further and further behind technology, it

becomes imperative to define the ethics by which future developers, hackers, creators, and

consumers operate in online spaces.



         As Sara Baase outlined in chapter 3 of A Gift of Fire: Social, Legal, and Ethical Issues

for Computing Technology, there are basic limits on what society deems acceptable under

freedom of speech laws. While pornography has been a topic of contention, it is widely

accepted that child pornography is not under the protection of the First Amendment.1 To the

extent that individuals feel safe online, their feelings should not matter as much as the

criminality of a post that endangers the subjects in the content or immediately threatens the

lives of others.2 To that point, there have been many cases backed by the ACLU supporting the

right of white nationalists, neo-nazis, and KKK members to hold rallies and marches. A couple

of the most famous Supreme Court rulings are the 1977 Skokie case and the 1993 Ex parte

Lowe case. In the Skokie case, David Goldberger (a Jewish Lawyer) and Aryeh Neier (the

ACLU’s national director and a Jewish refugee from Nazi Germany) defended the right of

neo-Nazis to march through Skokie, Illinois, a town with a massive population of Holocaust

survivors.3 They won the case. In the 1993 Ex parte Lowe case, an esteemed Black lawyer,

Anthony Griffin from the ACLU, represented the grand dragon of the KKK to protect his First

Amendment right to refuse a subpoena from the Texas Commission on Human Rights to turn

         In the modern age, there is virtually no major media/social-networking platform without

censorship of some kind.5 The depth and character of that censorship, however, is largely a

function of the economic leverage between the government and the dominant social platforms

operating within its borders. Since 2022, 10 governments have undergone significant civil

uprisings organized substantially through social media, with at least three – Sri Lanka,

Bangladesh, and Nepal – experiencing outright regime collapse. None of those countries had

strong pre-existing censorship agreements with the platforms that hosted the organizing and

upheaval. This was not incidental. In each case, the platforms had little to no economic

incentive to build compliance infrastructure with the governments whose user bases

represented a negligible share of global revenue.6 Nepal, in particular, gains more revenue

proportional to its GDP from the large social media companies (Meta and TikTok in particular)

than the large media companies gain from Nepal (relative to their global revenue).7 The

censorship that did occur was reactive, not structural. It was censorship imposed by

governments in a desperate attempt to quell movements that had already reached critical mass

on the platforms. This stands in sharp contrast to how platforms operate in high-leverage

markets. In the EU, the Digital Services Act compels proactive content governance backed by

the threat of massive fines.8 In India, IT Rules empower law enforcement to demand targeted

        The core question of freedom of speech lacks nuance in the age of the infinite scroll. You

can post 800 videos, but if the algorithm favors a different kind of content in your region, your

video may be less likely to be seen. In this instance, the question of freedom of speech vs

feeling safe takes on perhaps a different meaning. For example, there have been studies and

reports on the algorithm of Meta’s platforms that prioritize white creators over many other

demographics, not due to a natural interaction boosting algorithm, but rather due to a weighted

bias against certain types and colors of people. Leaked internal research from Meta validated

these findings, but their leadership chose to keep the algorithms in place to appease

conservative politicians in power and avoid any disruption.10 In that instance, does freedom of

speech come into view? How do you interpret freedom of speech with an infinite scroll platform

where users interface with a semi-random content algorithm, designed to maximize time and

attention from the user?11 If you are free to post anything and no one sees it, are you really

         These questions are probably best exemplified by the TikTok acquisition-merger, where

TikTok was deemed a national security threat in the United States, and was forced into a merger

with Oracle (and other American companies) to keep its US platform operational. One of the

major points of the ban bill that was proposed included concerns about the number of posts and

ratio of engagement with pro-Palestinian sentiment on the app, and that it was shaping younger

Americans’ views on the support of Israel and engagement with the Middle East.12 There were

multiple hearings (that went viral) with the CEO of TikTok for the congresspeople’s nonsensical

questions about the platform. A study was done at Northeastern University that actually

investigated the claims about bias towards pro-Palestinian content, and found that the platform

is “inherently majoritarian”, that there was no artificial boosting or demoting of Israeli or

Palestinian content – the pro-Palestine movement was a natural phenomenon.13 Meta, on the

other hand, had further leaked memos that indicated active algorithmic suppression of

pro-Palestine content, along with complying with 94% of censorship requests from Israeli

authorities since October 2023 (millions of posts).14



         The American TikTok saga starkly contrasts the controversial stances that civil rights

leaders and lawyers have taken in decades past to support the right to free speech. Its technical

nature was able to obscure the issue as a matter of national security when, really, the issue was

(and is) freedom of speech. The principles and values that we consider inalienable rights must
