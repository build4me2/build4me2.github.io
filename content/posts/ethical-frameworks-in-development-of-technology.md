                        Ethical Frameworks for Development in Technology



To paraphrase one of the most prolific and controversial tech investors: <a href="https://conversationswithbillkristol.org/transcript/peter-thiel-transcript/">“Technologies can grow

very quickly and outpace politics – innovative companies often find themselves in a gray legal

zone. Once enough people depend on the technology, shutting it down becomes politically

untenable — the politics gets dragged along.”</a>1 Thebounds of law on technology, or lack thereof,

are what interest me in the discussion of ethics and computer science. How can the creation of

products or tools be done ethically when the law fails to define bounds for the technology to

operate? When innovation specifically targets areas outside existing laws, does that result in an

ethical failure or success for the state or the consumer? The field of computer science, and more

broadly in technology, is littered with extreme examples of beneficial and harmful

implementations, ranging from genocide to grassroots non-violent movements and green

initiatives.


When discussing the ethics of computer science, it becomes imperative to clarify what the

technology of computation has come to mean in the modern age. Computation, compute, and

computer applications are themselves forms of information governance – almost always

operating on or outside the edge of defined law in any given nation-state. The process and

outcome of innovations in computer science are difficult to predict, which often makes virtue

ethics one of the most useful ethical frameworks concerning computer science development and

deployment.

Ethics more broadly encompasses a wide range of philosophies, with a range and scope of an

almost infinite set of hypothetical scenarios. The question of “what is ethical?” varies across the

different hypotheticals proposed (i.e., who would you pull the lever for on a train track, etc.).2

Ethics is more easily defined as a field regarding morality and governance than any one specific

meaning or definition. When ethics is combined with more materially relevant fields, such as

engineering, computer science, or supply chain logistics, it takes on a more poignant focus. The

impact and execution of physical implementation bring grounding to the field of ethics that it

perhaps does not have on its own.


The definitions of Deontology and Utilitarianism are difficult to square with current innovations

in software and technology. Utilitarianism makes sense; it is about maximizing aggregate

well-being, but the aggregate itself can be difficult to calculate when considering new technology

or software. In particular, I would argue it is nearly impossible to take a utilitarian approach to

developing software when the downstream effects have no definite conclusion. At best, you can

make a utilitarianpredictionabout how new softwarewill change people’s lives, but that has

more to do with optimism than it does ethics. A prime example of this is the AI industry as a

whole, which has so far had more negative impacts on the environment, government, and youth

than it has had positive. Yet billions of dollars continue to be poured in. The ethics of

utilitarianism don’t seem to be a particularly useful framework to grapple with questions in that

particular domain: in the beginning, LLMs and AI were posited as a utilitarian technology, one

that was offered for free because itshouldbe, andit would therefore benefit the largest number

of people. Its downstream effects on the internet, environment, and education have yet to display

a positive outcome in that regard.3 But people will not give up on that field, nor should they. The

utilitarian ethic can be applied in the process of development, but it doesn’t always yield the

results that it was aiming for. A deontological approach can be equally as difficult to work with

as an ethical framework when discussing the development of technology. Since it emphasizes the

universality of decisions and principles, regardless of outcome, there are strict rules that may

shift with more context. To use the same AI example, you could argue that providing free

artificial intelligence access is a deontological decision – it is simply the right thing to do when

not everyone has access to the same resources. The universal principle is that “it’s a duty to share

knowledge freely.” But payment comes in many forms, and those same companies have quietly

changed their terms of service to collect user data from free-tier users. Additionally, the glut of

AI availability has had catastrophic consequences on the environment and energy sectors, which

were known before the decision was made. While the deontological perspective may seem like a

good way to approach information governance, its rigidity can have catastrophic consequences

because often the real process of decision-making has enough complexity that basic universal

principles can not realistically be applied. Even if you weren’t to consider the consequences, the

decisions themselves are attached to global supply chains and energy infrastructure that cannot

be simplified down to a few principles.


The chaotic nature of global supply chains, distributed computing, and physical infrastructure is

more easily assessed in a framework similar to virtue ethics. Virtue ethics offers a more flexible

method for defining ethical action in a field like software development or computer science,

where innovation is rapid and usually pushing against the borders of laws. One of the core
